Since the [[Division of the World]], the people of the [[T'va Islands]] have been the most rigorous in keeping meticulous records of their history. While most other islands let the story of the Oceansky fall into myth, a woman named [[Ceyi of T'va]] rose up as the first [[Storykeeper]] only two years after the Division, devoting her life to seeking out information and ensuring that future generations would continue her work. Ceyi saw that oral tradition could only be reliable up to a certain point and developed her own form of writing, creating the first T'va Scrolls. It is widely believed that these scrolls were the earliest appearance of an organized, universal system of written language.

# Transcriptions

## Scroll One
> The words of [[Ceyi of T'va]], the first [[Storykeeper]], recorded here for future generations to ponder and not forget. Let there in each generation rise up new Storykeepers who dedicate their lives to the preserving of our history. Let them study the old stories, speak with those who witness great events, and record the words of their time so that those who take their place may continue the tradition.
> Hear now the story of the [[Division of the World]], for a day may come when children are born who have never known of a world with one great land and a never-setting sun. This is the world from which all life has come, and it is the world all life must return to at the time of the [[Rejoining of the World]]. For all time until this one, humanity was gathered in one place under one [[Great Sun]]. The sun never set over the world, but constantly provided light, warmth, and life to the world. Humankind lived in peace with one another for untold ages. However, as humanity developed on the world, so did their greed, ambition, and vanity. Though the land and its resources were plentiful, people began to fight over ownership, which led to a time of great war. For one hundred years this evil age continued, until the land itself was so grieved by humanity's actions that it split itself into a thousand pieces so that humankind would not be altogether destroyed by one another. The ground shook and water came upon it, and the land became islands which were cast away from one another until they became only distant shapes on the edge of the sea. As they drifted from each other, they soon drifted from the sun.
